# Challenge 3 · Steganography lvl 3

Well now… this here's a simple one, leastways it looks that way.
Just one lonely JPEG, sittin' there waitin' to be picked over.
Your job's plain enough — find the flag.
But I'll tell you, partner, there ain't much in this world that's as simple as it looks.
Plenty of folks've gone diggin' through fossils like this and never come back with so much as a whisper.

Now, if you get yourself stuck, and you ain't got nothin' better to do, lean back a spell, and I'll spin you a yarn about a feller they called John, back in the Desert Storm days.

They say John could stare down a locked file the way a cowboy sizes up a spooked horse — patient, calm, and just a little amused.
He wasn't ridin' solo, neither. Had a few sharp pals who shared his taste for codes and riddles — Aho, Weinberger, and Kernighan.
Together, they were somethin' of a posse — the kind who could turn scrambled lines into sense faster than a dust devil changes course.

When John needed a way to tie loose ends together, those three showed him a clever trick: how to stitch two strings — a word and a tail — into one clean rope.
He liked that trick so much he'd jot their initials — A W K — in the margins of his notebook, a quiet grin on his face, like he was leavin' himself a joke only he'd get.

John, he always found ways to make work into play.
He'd tell the young codebreakers, "Don't just pick a name — build one. Give it a backbone, then tack on somethin' small that makes it yours."
To prove his point, he'd show 'em how to make a password that wasn't just strong, but had a rhythm to it: start with a codename, add a `#`, then toss on two numbers and a three-letter tag that danced between lower and upper case.
Made sense to him — a little structure, a little flair, and a lesson tucked inside the challenge.

There was even a friend of his — a real peculiar fella — who wrote in fancy, alien-lookin' symbols, said you could describe any pattern with nothin' but question marks and letters.
Folks these days call it mask shorthand or somethin' close to it.
And if you peeked at John's old notebook, you'd see one of his patterns scribbled out like this:

```
?d?d?l?u?l
```

Well, I hope ya enjoyed an old man's tale about puzzles, little suffixes, and how sometimes the smallest patterns can open the biggest doors.
So go on now. The JPEG's waitin'.
